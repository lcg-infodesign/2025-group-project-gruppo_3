THE GRAPH: A MAP OF VOLCANIC POWER
This visualization transforms volcanic eruption data into an interactive map that tells the story of Earth's destructive power. Each black dot represents a real event, positioned on a large concentric circle. Its position is not random: it is determined by an Impact Score, a score between 1 and 16, calculated by adding five key indicators.
THE IMPACT SCORE SYSTEM
The Impact Score (from 1 to 16) is calculated by summing five indicators, each rated from 0 to 4:
  • DEATHS - From 1 to 4, based on the number of victims (from 1-50 to over 1,000).
  • INJURIES - From 1 to 4, based on injured people (from 1-50 to over 1,000).
  • DESTROYED HOUSES - From 1 to 4, based on lost buildings (from 1-50 to over 1,000).
  • ECONOMIC DAMAGE - From 1 to 4, based on estimated costs (from less than 1 million to over 25 million dollars).
  • MISSING - From 0 to 4, to indicate the number of missing or unidentified people (from 1-50 to over 1,000; 0 if not relevant or not documented).
An eruption with a score of 16 was devastating in all aspects; one with a score of 1, instead, had minimal impact.
HOW TO READ THE GRAPH
THE BLACK DOTS : Each dot represents a volcanic eruption. Hovering the mouse over a dot displays a window with the volcano's name and the eruption year. Clicking on the dot directly accesses the event's detail page, where you can explore the data further.
THE CONCENTRIC CIRCLES: The outer ring corresponds to score 1 (minimal impact), while the bright red central point represents the maximum score of 16 (maximum devastation). The closer a dot is to the center, the more severe the event was.
THE CONTINENTAL WEDGES: The circle is divided into five wedges, each representing a continent: Asia, Americas, Europe, Oceania, Africa. Within each wedge, eruptions are arranged clockwise, starting from the oldest event (4200 BC) to today.
EXTERNAL CONTROLS: FILTERS AND ANIMATION
On the left side of the page, tools are available to explore the data dynamically.
  • SELECT TIME FRAME - With the arrows << and >>, you can select a specific time interval (for example, "800 AD - 1800 AD"). The graph updates automatically, showing only eruptions that occurred in that period.
  • SELECT YEAR - With the arrows < and >, you can navigate year by year among those present in the dataset. Not all years are present, as the dataset does not include records for every single year. In recent decades, however, precision increases significantly, allowing analysis of individual years like 2005.
  • START ANIMATION - Starts an automatic animation that scrolls through available years in succession, highlighting eruptions that occurred in each year with a luminous pulsating effect (a red glow that expands around the dot).
